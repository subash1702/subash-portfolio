NYC Traffic Volume Analytics Pipeline

An analytics pipeline on 1.7M+ NYC Automated Traffic Volume Counts joined to the city’s Permitted Event Information feed. Exploratory analysis surfaces the patterns hiding in the raw counts; a regressor and three classifiers expose where the easy signal ends and what richer features would actually be needed to forecast urban congestion.

Pythonpandasscikit-learnXGBoostseaborn

What it does

  • Ingests two NYC Open Data feeds — 1.7M traffic counter readings and the permitted-events log — and parses heterogeneous timestamp columns into a single event-time index.
  • Cleans, normalizes, samples, and left-joins both feeds on (borough, date), attaching an is_event_time flag to every traffic row.
  • Runs a five-plot EDA pass: hourly profile, day-of-week, event vs non-event distribution, borough totals, and an hour×day heatmap.
  • Trains a Gradient Boosting Regressor on traffic volume and three classifiers (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost) on weekday vs weekend, then compares them on ROC-AUC and per-class precision and recall.

Architecture

Four stages, each independently runnable. The cleaning and merge stage is the load-bearing one — most of the analytical value of the pipeline comes from building a single trustworthy timestamp out of five separate integer columns and reconciling borough strings across two datasets that don’t agree on capitalization or whitespace. After that, EDA and modelling are straightforward consumers of one well-shaped dataframe.

Two feeds in parallel → cleaned, sampled, merged → fanned out to EDA, regression, and classification.

What I built

Ingestion and cleaning. The traffic feed splits date and time across five integer columns (Yr / M / D / HH / MM); the events feed carries one string column (Start Date/Time) in a different format. The first job of the pipeline is to make both speak the same language — a single pd.Timestamp on each row, plus derived hour_of_day, day_of_week, and is_weekend. Borough strings get trimmed and title-cased so " MANHATTAN " and "manhattan" stop being two different keys.

Sampling and merge. 25,000 traffic rows and 2,500 event rows are sampled with random_state=42 for iteration speed — the full 1.7M rows are tractable but slow to iterate against. A left join on (event_day, borough) attaches the events to the traffic, and a derived is_event_time flag carries forward into every downstream consumer.

EDA. Five plots, written as one function so the whole exploration runs from one call. The hourly profile shows a clean bimodal curve (morning ~8 AM, evening ~4–5 PM, trough 1–4 AM). The day-of-week bar chart puts Thursday and Friday at the top. The borough totals show Manhattan, Brooklyn, and Queens dominating; Staten Island is roughly one-fifth of Queens. The hour×day heatmap is the most useful — it shows the bimodal pattern collapsing on weekends into a flatter, lower curve.

Regressor. A scikit-learn GradientBoostingRegressor on [is_event_time, hour_of_day, day_of_week] predicting Vol. Train/test split 80/20, deterministic seed. The output goes into a scatter of actual vs predicted plus printed R² and MSE.

Classifiers. Three weekday-vs-weekend models on [Vol, hour_of_day, Boro_code]: LogisticRegression (on scaled features), RandomForestClassifier, and XGBClassifier. Each one is reported with a classification report, a confusion matrix, and a shared ROC plot so all three curves sit on the same axes for comparison.

What the models actually showed

The most useful thing the models did was tell me where the feature set ran out of signal.

Volume prediction. The Gradient Boosting Regressor on [is_event_time, hour_of_day, day_of_week] hit R² = 0.07 with MSE ≈ 30,472. That’s not a model failure — it’s the model correctly reporting that event presence alone isn’t enough. To actually forecast volume around events you’d need event type, expected attendance, venue proximity, and the specific street segment, none of which the current join carries.

Weekday vs weekend. Three classifiers, same features (Vol, hour, borough), same train/test split:

ModelROC-AUCRead
Logistic Regression 0.51 Effectively random — the signal isn’t linear in these features
Random Forest 0.76 Best balanced; picks up the hour×weekend interaction
XGBoost 0.71 High overall accuracy, but weekend recall drops to 0.24

Random Forest is the recommended model on this feature set. The gap between Logistic Regression and the tree models is the actual takeaway — there’s real signal there, but only in the interaction between hour and day-type, which a linear decision boundary can’t see.

Why it stands out

Most student traffic projects stop at one chart and one accuracy number. This one runs the full loop — messy CSV → reconciled dataframe → five-plot EDA → four models → honest report on what the features can and can’t support. The most important result is negative: a 1.7M-row dataset isn’t enough on its own, and the writeup says exactly which features would unlock the rest.

How I think about this kind of work

  • The merge is the model. Most of the analytical value lives in making two messy feeds speak the same language; everything downstream is a consumer.
  • Negative results are still results. A model that hits R² = 0.07 has told you something specific — that the features you gave it aren’t the features you need.
  • Always run a linear baseline alongside the tree models. The gap between Logistic Regression and Random Forest is what tells you whether the signal is linear or interactional.
  • EDA earns its weight before modelling does. A clear hour×day heatmap is worth more than a marginal AUC bump.
